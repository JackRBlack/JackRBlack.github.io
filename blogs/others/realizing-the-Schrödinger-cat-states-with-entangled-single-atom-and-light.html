Realizing the Schrödinger Cat States with Entangled Single Atom and Light

Wenjie Chen

Mar. 22nd, 2019

In spite of the vivid story Schrödinger made up to question the Copenhagen interpretation in 19351, the Schrödinger cat states are nowadays refered to as quantum states prepared in superpositions of classically distinguishable states2. Recently, Hacker et al. managed to create such cat states using a single atom and optical pulses3. In this review I will briefly introduce their work, and make some comparison between the original Schrödinger cat state and the one produced in their experiments. Some brief discussion about such experiments will be included as well.

However if the atom is in the "off" state, the light will be in the resonant state of the cavity and thus get in and pick up an additional phase.

Biggest Challenge to Realize the Schrödinger Cat States

The biggest challenge to realize such Schrödinger cat states in experiments is isolating the system from the environmental perturbations. Due to the interaction with the environment, the coherent superposition pure states will decohere into classical mixed states. If the isolation is not good enough, this process will happen nearly instantaneously. The more "classical" (e.g. bigger) the system is, the quicker this process happens. This is why it's so hard to realize the real cat and macroscopic superposition state, while it's possible to realize the light and mesoscopic superposition state.

Importance of This Work

The method to produce these Schrödinger cat states may serve multiple purposes:

  • Quantum control of classical states. The work demonstrate an excellent method to control classical light with quantum variables (i.e. single atom). This may serve as a quantum gate.
  • Optical based quantum computation. The superposition of coherent state can serve as qubits. The advantage of such qubits is that it's classically distinguishable. The challenge is to reduce the losses further.
  • Quantum internet. The produced superposition cat states can travel in fibers, make it easy to control and distribute. The challenge is to reduce the losses further.
